Trust & Transparency

Why Local-First

Updated April 2026
The short version: your financial data lives on your phone, not on our servers. We don't have a copy of your transactions to leak, sell, or hand over.

What “local-first” means here

When you add a transaction, set a budget, or import a statement, that information is stored on your device in a private database. It isn't sent to a JosspaTech server. We don't have a master copy somewhere — we genuinely don't know what you spent at the grocery store last week, and we don't want to.

This is different from how most personal-finance apps work. Most use a cloud-first model where your data is uploaded to the company's servers, indexed, analyzed, and synced back to your devices. That model is convenient, but it means there's a centralized copy of your financial life sitting in someone else's database.

What this protects you from

  • Data breaches. If we got hacked tomorrow, attackers wouldn't find your transactions on our servers because they aren't there.
  • Subpoenas and broad requests. We can't hand over data we don't hold.
  • Internal misuse. No JosspaTech employee can scroll through your spending out of curiosity. Nobody can.
  • Re-purposing. Your data won't be quietly used to train models, build advertising profiles, or feed lead-gen partnerships.

Where data does leave the device

We're not pretending the app runs on a deserted island. There are a few places where data crosses the wire, all of which are opt-in or limited:

  • Cloud OCR (off by default). If you turn this on, blurry receipt scans get sent to a third-party OCR service over TLS to be read more accurately. The image isn't retained by the provider.
  • AI features (off by default). Optional AI features send aggregated patterns — not raw transactions — to our backend for analysis. Raw amounts and merchants stay on-device.
  • Crowd-sourced reference data (opt-out). Anonymized aggregate signals like “most-used name for this barcode” or “most-common category for this merchant” flow into a shared reference that helps every user. There's nothing personal in that contribution.
  • Backups you choose. If you turn on iCloud, Google Drive, or another backup, the encrypted backup goes to your storage, not ours.
  • Sync with a partner you choose. If you set up household sync with a partner, an encrypted snapshot is exchanged peer-to-peer or via a relay we run. Even there, our servers don't keep your data — the relay forwards encrypted bytes and forgets them.

What this costs you

Local-first is honest about its trade-offs. The big one is that you're responsible for your data: if you lose your phone and don't have a backup turned on, the data goes with it. We've built backup, restore, and partner-sync to make this easy — but we can't recover data from our servers because we never had it.

We think that's the right trade. The alternative is convenience at the cost of giving someone else permanent custody of your financial life.

Why don't more apps do it this way? Local-first is harder to build, harder to monetize, and harder to support than cloud-first. There's no way to mine the data for second revenue streams, no way to A/B-test on aggregated server logs, and no easy “just look it up for them” in customer support. It's a deliberate constraint — and it's the constraint that makes the privacy promise real instead of marketing.
