The Download-Arrow Workflow

A lot of stores email you a PDF receipt instead of a paper one. Pocket Budget can pull those into your budget without scanning your whole inbox. You just download the PDF to your phone the normal way, and Pocket Budget handles the rest.

Slide 2 of 7

Open the Receipt Email

Open the email from the store. Scroll down to the attachment. Here we’re showing Gmail — you’ll see the file name, the size, and a download arrow right next to the PDF. Other email apps like Outlook, Apple Mail, Yahoo, or Proton look a little different, but every one of them has a way to save the attachment to your phone.

Slide 3 of 7

Tap the Download Arrow

Tap the download arrow. In Gmail it sits right on the attachment. In other email apps, you may need to tap the attachment first to reveal the save or download icon — sometimes it’s behind a share button. The icon changes, but every email app lets you save a file to your phone. You’re just saving it. Not opening it, not sharing it.

Slide 4 of 7

The PDF Saves Locally

Your phone puts the file in your Downloads folder. Nothing leaves your device. No cloud upload, no sharing with anyone. The PDF just sits on your phone waiting for Pocket Budget to grab it.

Slide 5 of 7

Open Pocket Budget

Open Pocket Budget. Tap the gold plus button at the bottom center of the Transactions screen, then tap Import receipts. Only a few taps to get there — and from then on, one tap is all you need.

Slide 6 of 7

Automatic Discovery

Pocket Budget checks your Downloads folder for receipts you haven’t imported yet. PDFs, photos of paper receipts, even plain-text receipt emails you saved — if it’s in there and it’s new, Pocket Budget will find it. Files you’ve already imported or told Pocket Budget to skip won’t show up again.

Slide 7 of 7

Confirm, then choose: keep a copy?

Pocket Budget reads the PDF and shows you a draft with the merchant, date, and total. Tweak anything you want, tap confirm, and it’s in your budget. Then Pocket Budget asks: do you want to keep a copy of the receipt? Say yes and the full PDF goes to your own Cloud Backup — iCloud, Google Drive, or Dropbox. A small thumbnail always stays on your phone so you can see what the receipt looks like at a glance. If Cloud Backup isn’t set up yet, Pocket Budget walks you through it — because without it, kept receipts could be lost if something happens to your phone.

Why This Is the Safer Way

1

You Pick Each File

PocketBudJet never scrolls through your inbox or reads your messages. You download the specific receipt you want, the app sees only that file.

2

Works with Any Email Provider

Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, Proton, your work email — doesn’t matter. If you can download an attachment, you can feed it into your budget.

3

No Login, No OAuth, No Waiting

You don’t sign into anything. No permissions to grant, no approval screens. The PDF just lands in Downloads and PocketBudJet reads it from there.

4

Same Flow for Photos of Paper Receipts

Snap a photo of a paper receipt, save it to your phone, and PocketBudJet finds it the same way. One import button handles PDFs, photos, and scanned files.

5

Pocket Budget Asks Before Keeping

After PocketBudJet pulls the data from a receipt, it asks: do you want to keep a copy? Say no and only the parsed transaction is saved. Say yes and the full PDF goes to your own Cloud Backup — iCloud, Google Drive, or Dropbox. Either way, a small thumbnail stays on your phone so you can always see what each receipt looked like.

6

Cloud Backup Keeps Receipts Safe

If you want PocketBudJet to keep your receipts, set up Cloud Backup — otherwise, kept receipts live only on your phone and could be lost if it’s damaged, stolen, or replaced. See how to set up Cloud Backup. PocketBudJet will remind you if you start keeping receipts without it.

Why we built it this way

The other approach — an app that reads your Gmail for you — requires handing over permission to see every email in your inbox. PocketBudJet refuses to ask for that. You pick each receipt. The app only ever sees what you hand it. That’s the whole privacy pitch in one sentence.

The two folders, two behaviors

PocketBudJet reads two folders. The Downloads folder is yours — PocketBudJet pulls the data from receipts you downloaded but always asks before keeping a copy or cleaning up. The PocketBudJet Import folder is for documents you have stored elsewhere (like bank statements you downloaded from your bank’s website) — PocketBudJet pulls the data and dumps the file, since you already have the original at the source. Drop bank statements in Import. Receipts come in through Downloads.
